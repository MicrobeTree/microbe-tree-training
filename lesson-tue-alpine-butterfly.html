What Is the Alpine Butterfly?

The alpine butterfly knot (also called the lineman's loop or butterfly loop) creates a fixed, isolated loop in the middle of a rope without needing access to either end. It's a mid-line loop knot — you form it in the standing part of the rope while both ends remain in use.

Unlike a figure-8 on a bight (which requires a free end or doubling back), the butterfly is the go-to knot when you need a loop anywhere along a rope that's already anchored at both ends.

  • Classification: Fixed loop knot; mid-line; symmetrical loading
  • Also known as: Lineman's loop, butterfly loop, lineman's rider
  • Key property: The loop remains stable under load from the standing end on either side, or from the loop itself — true tri-directional loading capability
  • Knot efficiency: Approximately 50–60% of rope break strength (the knot reduces rope strength by creating a stress concentration at the bend)
  • Ease of untying: Unties cleanly after loading — minimal jamming compared to figure-8 variants
📋 Field Note

The alpine butterfly earns its keep on jobs where you're mid-rope and need a clip-in point, a redirect anchor, or you need to isolate a damaged section — all without untying from your anchor. You can't do that with most other loop knots. It's not the most common knot in the kit, but when you need it, nothing else will do.

Method 1 — The Hand-Wrap Method (Fastest in Field)

This is the preferred method for tying a butterfly in an already-rigged system where you can't access the ends. Takes about 15 seconds once you have the motion.

Method 2 — Overhand Loop Method (Most Reliable for Learning)

Step 1

Form a large loop in the rope at the desired location. Hold the crossing point.

Step 2

Twist the crossing point twice in the same direction to create two half-hitches stacked on top of each other.

Step 3

Take the loop that's been formed on one side and bring it up and over the top of both twists.

Step 4

Push that loop down through the center gap between the two twisted sections. Pull the loop through.

Step 5

Dress firmly by pulling both standing ends while holding the loop. The knot barrel should seat symmetrically.

Verify

Pull the loop in three directions — standing-end-1, standing-end-2, and the loop itself. It should not slip or change shape in any direction.

Arboricultural Applications

Mid-line redirect anchor

Create a fixed loop anywhere along a lowering line to redirect force through a block or redirect pulley — without cutting the rope.

Isolating a damaged rope section

If a rope section is abraded, burned, or compromised mid-line, tie a butterfly on each side of the damage and use only the loop — the damaged section bears no load.

Three-point load distribution

In guying or multi-anchor rigging setups, a butterfly in the center of a line allows load to be shared across two directions while the loop takes a third attachment.

Foot ascender loop

Some climbers tie an alpine butterfly into their footloop line — provides a fixed-size, stable, adjustable-position loop that won't slip under body weight.

Haul system tethering

When setting up a z-rig or 3:1 haul, a butterfly provides a fixed attachment point on the haul line that won't migrate under repeated loading/release cycles.

Rope-length management

On an overlong rope, tie a butterfly and clip the loop to the anchor — effectively shortening the working rope by a fixed amount without cutting.

⚠ When NOT to Use the Alpine Butterfly

Not appropriate as a climbing hitch (it doesn't grip a running line). Do not use as a substitute for a prussik or VT hitch in a secondary tending system. Do not use as a terminal attachment — the figure-8 on a bight or bowline is the standard for terminal loops. Per ISA BMP Rigging, always select the knot appropriate to its specific load vector and application — there is no universal knot.

📋 Field Note

The rope-isolation use case is a job-saver. If you're mid-removal and find a section of your lowering line that's questionable — a heat mark, abrasion from a bark inclusion, bark imprint that went deeper than it looked — you don't have to pull the whole rope and restart. Tie butterflies on either side of the suspect section, clip through the loops with a carabiner instead of using the line directly, and continue. That's a field judgment call that requires knowing the knot cold.

Load Characteristics & Knot Efficiency

Understanding knot efficiency is not academic — it directly determines whether your rigging system is inside or outside WorkSafeBC-mandated safety margins.

  • Knot efficiency (~50–65%): A butterfly reduces the break strength of the rope at the knot to approximately 50–65% of the rope's catalog break strength. Example: 13mm arborist climbing rope with 27 kN break strength = ~14–18 kN at the butterfly.
  • Why knots weaken rope: The bend radius created by the knot induces a stress concentration. As the rope exits the knot body, fibers on the outside of the bend bear more load than fibers on the inside — this uneven loading initiates failure at a lower overall force.
  • Direction symmetry: The butterfly has nearly equal efficiency regardless of which direction the load comes from (standing-end-1, standing-end-2, or the loop) — this is unique among mid-line loops and is why it's specified for multi-directional applications.
  • Comparative efficiency: Figure-8 on a bight: ~55–75% · Bowline: ~60–75% · Overhand loop: ~45–55% · Alpine Butterfly: ~50–65%. The butterfly is slightly less efficient than a well-formed figure-8, but significantly more so than an overhand loop.
  • Dynamic vs. static: Arborist work generates shock loads — a piece dropped and arrested generates forces far exceeding its static weight. ANSI Z133 requires rigging systems rated for the anticipated dynamic forces, not just the piece weight. Factor shock loading into your system design.
  • Minimum WLL protocol: Per WorkSafeBC OHS Regulation Part 15, rigging components must have a documented working load limit. Use the weakest component in the system — including knot-reduced rope strength — as your system WLL.
⚠ Safety Margin Requirement

ANSI Z133 does not specify a single safety factor for all rigging, but WorkSafeBC and industry practice use a minimum 5:1 safety factor (WLL = break strength ÷ 5) for rigging systems with dynamic loading. With a butterfly in a 27 kN rope: effective WLL = ~14 kN ÷ 5 = 2.8 kN (~285 kg). Always know your numbers before you load the system.

📋 Field Note

The safety factor math is something you should be able to do on a job site with a piece of chalk on the truck door. Rope break strength (from the manufacturer tag, in kN) × knot efficiency (0.55 for a butterfly, conservative) ÷ safety factor (5) = your working load limit for the knotted system. If your piece exceeds that, you need a mechanical advantage system, a larger-diameter rope, or a different attachment method. Do the math before the cut, not after.

Pre-Use Inspection Checklist

Per ANSI Z133 Section 9 and WorkSafeBC OHS Regulation, all rigging components including knots must be inspected before each use. Here's the butterfly-specific check sequence:

  • ✓Rope condition at knot entry/exit points — no abrasion, heat marks, chemical exposure, or discoloration. These stress concentration points are where failure initiates.
  • ✓Knot barrel symmetry — the butterfly should look the same on both sides. Asymmetry means it wasn't fully dressed or the wrong knot was tied.
  • ✓Loop geometry — the loop should be round or oval, not twisted or crossed. A twisted loop alters how the load distributes through the knot body.
  • ✓Tri-directional pull test — apply light hand tension from each of the three directions (both standing ends and the loop). No slipping, no shape change.
  • ✓Correct identification — confirm this is actually a butterfly and not an accidental overhand loop (the overhand is weaker and does not have tri-directional stability). The butterfly has a specific barrel structure.
  • ✗Never use a loaded butterfly without inspection — once a knot has been loaded, re-inspect before re-loading. Dynamic loading can reveal latent dressing errors invisible before first load.
  • ✗Never dress a knot while it is loaded — the knot must be completely slack before dressing or adjusting. Adjusting a loaded knot can cause catastrophic failure.
  • ✗Never use a knot tied in webbing as a rope substitute — different material, different friction, different failure mode. The butterfly's efficiency ratings apply to round rope only.
📋 Field Note

The most common butterfly failure mode in arborist applications is not the knot itself — it's tying a different knot and calling it a butterfly. An overhand on a bight looks superficially similar but has significantly lower efficiency and does not have tri-directional load stability. Know what a correctly tied, fully dressed butterfly looks like. Tie it a hundred times until the barrel pattern is automatic recognition. Then you'll catch the wrong knot before it goes into the system.
