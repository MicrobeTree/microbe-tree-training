Blake's Hitch

The workhorse friction hitch of tree climbing — how to tie it, how it works, when to trust it, and when to back it up.

⊕ Hitch Diagram

⊕ Step-by-Step: Tying the Blake's Hitch
1
Orient your hitch cord alongside the host (climbing) rope. The working end of the hitch cord will form the load strand. Hold it alongside the rope with 30–40 cm of working end below your hand.
2
Begin wrapping the working end around the host rope, moving from bottom to top. Wrap toward yourself — crossing over the front of the host rope on each pass. Keep wraps tight and even, not overlapping.
3
Complete 4 full wraps around the host rope (some climbers use 5 for thinner cords or more friction). Each wrap should sit just above the previous one.
4
After the 4th wrap, pass the working end back down through the space between the host rope and the bottom (1st) wrap — from the front side, back to the load direction. This locks the hitch geometry.
5
The working/load end should now exit below the bottom wrap, pointing in the same direction as the load (downward). Attach this end to your saddle's bridge via a carabiner. The free/braking end exits above the top wrap.
6
Test before committing weight: Push the hitch up the rope (it should slide freely), then apply weight (it should grip immediately). If it slips under load before you tend the free end, add a wrap or check cord diameter compatibility.

Rule of thumb: Hitch cord should be 60–75% of the host rope's diameter. Always verify with your hitch cord and rope manufacturer's specifications — ratios vary by material and construction.

⊕ When NOT to Use the Blake's Hitch
⚠ Limitations — Know These Cold
  • As a standalone primary life-safety connection — the Blake's is a positioning/friction device, not a fall arrest system. Always maintain a secondary connection when the hitch is your only attachment.
  • On ropes of incompatible diameter — too similar and it slips; too dissimilar and it locks up and won't release. Test before full weight commitment on any new rope/cord combination.
  • When rope is wet, muddy, or icy — reduced friction means the hitch may not grip reliably. More wraps may compensate partially, but inspect and test carefully.
  • When the hitch will be loaded without tending — the Blake's must be manually managed during descent. Unlike a mechanical device (e.g., ROPE RUNNER, HITCH HIKER), it does not self-lock when you release the free end. It will slip.
  • On ropes that are heavily worn, fuzzy, or damaged — inconsistent surface texture leads to inconsistent grip. Retire damaged rope.
  • When ANSI Z133 Section 5 or your employer's safe work plan requires a mechanical device for the specific task — e.g., in high-risk rigging scenarios where both hands must be occupied.
📐 ANSI Z133 — What the Standard Says

ANSI Z133 Section 5.3 (Climbing Systems) requires that climbers working aloft use a friction hitch or mechanical device for controlled descent. The Blake's hitch meets this requirement when properly tied, on compatible ropes, and correctly tended. Section 5.3.6 additionally requires a secondary attachment point when working aloft — meaning the Blake's hitch alone (as a positioning device) is not a substitute for a second connection to the system.

📋 Field Note — Okanagan

In hot dry conditions common to Okanagan summers — above 35°C, low humidity — rope materials behave differently than in temperate climates. Polyester and polyblend climbing lines become stiffer; some hitch cords grip more aggressively. If you're climbing a ponderosa pine on a ridge site in July heat and your Blake's is binding rather than sliding cleanly, check that both rope and cord are within temperature spec for the materials. Also inspect for resin contamination — pine resin on the host rope changes friction dramatically and can cause either slipping (on fresh resin) or binding (on dried resin). Clean ropes before climbing in heavy resin conditions.
