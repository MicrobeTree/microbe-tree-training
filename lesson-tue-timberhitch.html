Timber Hitch & Killick Hitch
The grip that tightens when you pull — and releases when you don't
🪚 Tuesday — Equipment & Knots

🔍 What These Knots Are

The Timber Hitch is one of the oldest and most reliable knots in the arborist's toolkit — not because it's elegant, but because it does one thing brilliantly: it grips a spar, log, or branch the harder you pull it. It's a friction-based self-cinching hitch that loads into the wood, not around itself.

The Killick Hitch is the timber hitch with a half hitch added toward the leading end of the load. That single addition gives you directional control — without it, a log dangles vertically from the timber hitch end; with it, the load rides at an angle that makes dragging and lowering far more controllable.

These are temporary load hitches, not permanent attachments. They are specifically designed to release when tension is removed — which makes them fast and efficient in the field, but means you never use them where the rope must hold a load through dynamic cycles or slack periods.

🪢 Tying the Timber Hitch

Step 1

Pass the working end under and around the spar, then back over the standing part, forming a basic loop.

Step 2

Bring the working end back and begin wrapping it around its own standing part (these are "dogged turns").

Step 3

Complete 3 to 5 dogged turns, wrapping toward the direction of pull. More turns = more grip.

Complete

Under tension, the standing part cinches the loop down onto the spar. More load = tighter grip. Release tension to remove.

🪢 Adding the Killick (Half Hitch for Control)

The timber hitch alone holds and grips, but a log will dangle vertically or pivot unpredictably during lowering. The Killick Hitch solves this by adding a single half hitch placed 60–90 cm toward the leading end of the log (the end you want to point in the direction of travel or descent).

  • The timber hitch provides grip at one end
  • The half hitch (the killick addition) lifts the opposite end, controlling the angle of the load
  • Position the half hitch at about 60–90 cm from the timber hitch end for a standard log section; adjust for balance
  • The half hitch does not grip on its own — it relies on the timber hitch holding the load while the half hitch controls orientation
📋 Field Note — Okanagan

In summer ponderosa pine or cottonwood removal work, you'll frequently use the killick hitch to rig large wood sections off a crane line or block system for controlled descent. The timber hitch alone will let the section spin and pendulum — the killick addition keeps the butt angled away from the crew during the lower. When rigging heavy stem sections in Okanagan hardwood work (cottonwood, black locust), get the half hitch positioned for balance before the section goes into free space.

✅ When to Use / ❌ When Not To

✅ Dragging logs/sections
Cinches under the sustained tension of dragging — the ideal use case. Use killick hitch for directional control.
✅ Lowering stem sections
Reliable under constant rigging load. Release instantly when lowered to ground and tension removed.
✅ Starting a lashing
Traditional use in rope lashings for tripods and A-frames — begins with a timber hitch on the first spar.
✅ Securing a log for cutting
Quick, tool-free way to hold a log before bucking — especially useful with synthetic rope on rough timber.
❌ Sustained vertical hang
Loosens when tension is released or cycled. Never use as a permanent hanging attachment.
❌ Smooth synthetic rope
Dogged turns can slip on low-friction polyester or Dyneema. Use only on natural fibre or high-friction ropes.
❌ Dynamic climbing loads
Not designed for the dynamic tension cycles of climbing rope systems. Use only for static rigging applications.
❌ Replacing a proper sling
Where a rated sling and shackle are specified (per ISA BMP Rigging), use them — timber hitch is not rated for those applications.

⚠️ Critical: Pre-Use Rope Inspection

Per ANSI Z133 and ISA BMP for Rigging, inspect all rope before each use. Look for: cut or abraded fibres, internal core damage (rope feels flat or lumpy), discolouration from chemical exposure, kinking or hockle formation, or inspection-tag expiry. Any rope failing inspection must be removed from service immediately. A timber hitch on a compromised rope transfers no load safely — the knot is only as good as the rope holding it.

🧠 Field Tips

  • Direction matters: Wrap your dogged turns in the direction of pull — the friction cinches with load rather than against it. Reverse-wrapped turns can slip under heavy loads.
  • More turns on irregular wood: Rough-barked timber (ponderosa, Douglas-fir) provides good friction with 3 turns. Smooth-peeled or frozen logs may need 5 or more. When in doubt, add turns — there's no downside to extra wraps.
  • Killick placement for balance: Clip or wrap at roughly one-third from the heavy end of the section if you want near-horizontal control during lowering. Adjust by feel before the piece leaves the trunk.
  • Release confirmation: After lowering and slack, confirm release before re-rigging. A timber hitch that has carried heavy load under wet conditions can stick briefly — give the standing part a firm shake.
  • Not a rated sling: The timber hitch has no rated Working Load Limit (WLL) like a wire rope or synthetic sling does. For any rigging where a formal WLL calculation is required (crane operations, overhead rigging above crew), use rated hardware per ISA BMP Rigging specs.
📋 Field Note — Okanagan

The timber hitch and killick are most useful in the ground-operation phase — getting bucked sections to the chipper or across awkward terrain. In a West Kelowna strata removal where the access is a 2-metre gate and a narrow lawn corridor, you're often hand-dragging 40–80 kg sections with a block and tackle or a simple timber hitch on 16 mm bull rope. Get the dogged turns right, set your killick for directional pull, and that section slides cleanly. Skip the hitch and you're wrestling round wood that rolls every way except the one you want.
