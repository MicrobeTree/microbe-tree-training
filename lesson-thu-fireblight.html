Fire Blight — Erwinia amylovora

Fire blight is the most damaging bacterial disease of ornamental and fruit trees in the Rosaceae family — and the Okanagan, with its crabapples, hawthorns, mountain ash, and thousands of backyard apple trees, has plenty of hosts. Learn to recognize it in the field, understand what drives outbreaks, and know the pruning protocol cold. You will see this every spring.

📋 Quick Reference — Fire Blight

Pathogen: Erwinia amylovora — Gram-negative bacterium. Family Erwiniaceae.

Host range: Rosaceae family only. Common Okanagan hosts:

Apple (Malus)Pear (Pyrus)Crabapple (Malus)Hawthorn (Crataegus)Mountain ash (Sorbus)Saskatoon (Amelanchier)CotoneasterFirethorne (Pyracantha)

Key Symptoms

🦯 Shepherd's Crook

The pathognomonic sign: shoot tips wilt and bend into a hook shape. New growth wilts from the tip back while still attached. First and most obvious symptom.

💧 Bacterial Exudate

Amber, water-soaked ooze (bacterial ooze) on infected tissue in humid conditions — especially visible on cankers in cool, moist weather. Highly infectious.

🍂 Watersoaking & Blight

Infected blossoms and young fruit turn brown/tan and look water-soaked before drying. "Fire blight" — looks like the shoot was scorched.

⬇️ Canker Advance

Bacteria move from shoot tips down into larger branches. Canker margin shows discolored streaking in the cambium — reddish-brown streaks when you cut into the bark at the advancing margin.

Fire blight is one of the more consequential diagnoses you'll make in the Okanagan — get it wrong and you either spread the disease with your tools, or your client loses a mature crabapple they've had for 30 years. The shepherd's crook, the ooze, the cut depth — lock those in.
