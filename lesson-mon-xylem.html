Xylem & the Cohesion-Tension Mechanism

Water moves from roots to the tops of 40-metre trees without a pump. Understanding how is not just academic — it's the mechanistic foundation fos understanding drought stress, cavitation, soil compaction effects, root damage, and why some pruning timing matters more than you might think. This lesson covers the physics of water movement in trees and what it means when that system fails under Okanagan summer conditions.

📋 Quick Reference — The Water Transport Pathway

Soil water → Root hairs & mycorrhizaeOsmotic uptake; water moves from high to low water potential
↓
Root cortex → Endodermis (Casparian strip)Casparian strip forces water through living cells — selective filter
↓
Xylem vessels / tracheidsWater column under tension — cohesion holds molecules together
↓
Leaves → Stomata → AtmosphereTranspiration pulls the column upward — the engine of the whole system
ConceptPlain Language
Cohesion-tensionWater molecules stick to each other (cohesion). Evaporation at leaves creates negative pressure (tension) that pulls the whole column up from below.
Water potential (Ψ)A measure of water's energy — water always moves from higher Ψ (less negative) to lower Ψ (more negative). Soil to leaf is a gradient of decreasing Ψ.
Cavitation / embolismThe water column snaps under excessive tension — an air bubble enters the conduit and breaks the continuous column, blocking flow in that vessel or tracheid.
Tracheids vs vesselsTracheids (conifers) are individual cells connected through bordered pits — slower but safer under tension. Vessel elements (hardwoods) have open end walls — faster flow, more cavitation risk.

Water transport is the hydraulic system of the tree. Every time you see a wilting crown, yellowing needles in August, or a dying branch after root damage — this mechanism is what failed. Review the quick-reference until the pathway is automatic.
