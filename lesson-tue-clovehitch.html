The Clove Hitch — Quick, Adjustable, Situational

The clove hitch is one of the fastest knots to tie in arboricultural work and one of the most abused. It's a positioning tool — not a primary life safety anchor. Learning to tie it correctly in both configurations, understanding what it does well and where it rolls under load, and knowing the exact situations where something else is the better choice: that's what this lesson covers.

🪢 Two Methods — Over an Object vs. In the Bight

Method 1: Over an Object (End)
Used when you can access the end of the rope and pass it over or around the object (spar, branch, anchor). The most common method in tree climbing.
  • 1Pass the working end over and around the object — forming a simple turn
  • 2Pass the working end over and around again, this time crossing over the first wrap
  • 3Tuck the working end under the second wrap (the X) before it exits
  • 4Dress by pulling both ends firmly; the cross sits tight on the object
Method 2: In the Bight (Middle of Rope)
Tied without using the rope end — formed in the middle of a rope and dropped over an object. Faster in some rigging setups; requires access to the top of the anchor.
  • 1Form two identical overhand loops in the rope — same size, same orientation
  • 2Rotate the right loop toward you so it faces the opposite direction from the left
  • 3Nest the two loops together — the overhand cross of each loop becomes the clove hitch cross
  • 4Drop the combined loops over the object and dress by pulling both ends
📋 Tie It One-Handed

The clove hitch over an object can be tied with one hand — a critical skill for positioning a lanyard while aloft. Practice: hold the object with your non-dominant hand, pass the rope with your dominant hand only. Both wraps, the tuck, the dress — one hand. That's the standard. If you can't do it in gloves, you're not done practicing.

📌 Securing a Clove Hitch Against Rollout

Any time a clove hitch is used where the load direction may alternate — including most rigging applications — add two half-hitches around the standing part after the hitch, or use a stopper knot. The half-hitches convert the clove hitch into a clove hitch + half hitches (sometimes called a "cleat hitch" configuration) that resists rollout without adding meaningful bulk.

✅ Arborist Applications

🌳
Positioning lanyard anchor
Clipping or tying a positioning lanyard to a stem or branch union to hold a work position. Fast, adjustable. Add backup if the position will be weighted for extended periods.
🔀
Friction saver / redirect
Attaching a redirect rope to a branch or stem to change rope direction. Load is typically unidirectional; well-suited to clove hitch.
🔧
Tool and equipment attachment
Tying tools to a work line for haul-up. Fast to apply and release; load is usually unidirectional (tool weight only).
🪵
Temporary spar tie-off
Securing a rope to a spar or stem for temporary load. Useful during rigging operations for position holds. Add half-hitches if load will cycle.

🚫 When NOT to Use the Clove Hitch

Primary life safety anchor
ANSI Z133 requires life safety anchors to be rated hardware or knots appropriate for dynamic and static loading. The clove hitch's potential to roll under alternating load makes it unsuitable as a sole life safety tie-in. Use hardware, a bowline with backup, or a secure hitch with backup for primary anchors.
Bi-directional or alternating load
Any rigging scenario where the load will pull in more than one direction — swinging piece, rope under variable tension from both ends — risks rolling the clove hitch off the anchor. Add half-hitches or switch to a more stable hitch (timber hitch, round turn + 2 half-hitches).
On smooth or tapered objects
The clove hitch grips by friction on the object surface. On very smooth metal pipe, slick synthetic spars, or tapered objects, the hitch can roll or slip under load more easily than on rough bark or textured rope. Use a round turn before the hitch to increase friction area.
As a friction hitch
The clove hitch is a binding hitch — it grips an object. It is not a friction hitch (Prusik, VT, Blake's) and does not self-arrest on a rope under load. Do not use it where you need a self-locking, adjustable grip on a running line.
📋 Field Note — Okanagan Context

The most common misuse of the clove hitch in residential arborist work is using it as the only attachment for a climbing line anchor while doing a rigging operation on a dead ponderosa. Live-load rigging with a swinging piece creates exactly the bi-directional loading that can cause a bare clove hitch to roll. If you're rigging heavy sections on a dead Okanagan conifer with unpredictable wood failure, your anchor system needs more than a clove hitch — round turn plus two half-hitches, or hardware with a rated WLL, plus a redundant backup anchor.
