Felling Notch Geometry & Hinge Wood

The notch controls the fall. Understanding the geometry of the three notch types — conventional, open face, and Humboldt — and the hinge wood requirements behind them is foundational to controlled directional felling under ANSI Z133. Get the notch wrong and the hinge breaks early; get it right and the tree goes where you planned it to go.

📐 The Three Notch Types

Conventional Notch
Standard
Face opening: 45°
Top cut: horizontal
Bottom cut: 45° up to apex
Depth: 1/4–1/3 of trunk diameter
Back cut: level, 25–50 mm above notch base

Most common teaching notch. Adequate for routine felling. The hinge disengages earlier in the fall compared to open face.
Open Face Notch
Preferred for Control
Face opening: 70–90°
Top cut: angles slightly downward
Bottom cut: angles steeply upward
Depth: 1/4–1/3 of trunk diameter
Back cut: level at apex height

The hinge stays engaged longer during fall — the tree pivots on the hinge rather than sliding off it. Gives significantly more directional control. Preferred by TCIA and ISA for precision felling.
Humboldt Notch
Timber / Production
Face opening: 45° (inverted)
Top cut: horizontal
Bottom cut: angled upward to apex (inverted)
Purpose: minimizes butt wood waste — the notch piece falls away below, preserving the bottom of the trunk

Used in production logging to recover maximum timber volume. Not recommended for most arborist felling — harder to execute correctly, provides less directional control than open face.
📌 Which Notch to Use in Arborist Work

For the vast majority of tree removals — residential, ornamental, hazard trees — use the open face notch(070–90°). It keeps the hinge engaged longer, gives you more time to react during the fall, and provides better directional control. The conventional notch is adequate for straightforward felling but is being phased out of best practice in favor of open face. The Humboldt notch is a timber harvesting tool; leave it there.

📏 Hinge Wood — Dimensions & Requirements

The hinge is the uncut wood between the notch apex and the back cut. It is the control mechanism: it guides the fall, keeps the tree on its intended path, and prevents the butt from kicking back. Getting hinge dimensions right is not optional — too thin and the hinge breaks before you can steer; too thick and the back cut doesn't leave enough room.

ElementDimension / RequirementSource
Hinge width (each side)Approximately 10% of the trunk diameter. A 40 cm DBH tree = ~4 cm hinge per side.ANSI Z133; TCIA
Hinge thickness (notch depth)Notch depth: 1/4 to 1/3 of trunk diameter. This positions the hinge wood in the structurally strongest zone.ANSI Z133
Back cut height (conventional notch)Back cut at least 25–50 mm (1–2 inches) above the base of the notch — creates the step that prevents the butt from kicking back.ANSI Z133 Section 8.3
Back cut height (open face notch)Back cut made at or slightly above the notch apex height — the geometry of the large face opening provides the kick-back prevention.TCIA / ISA open face felling standards
Holding wood (remaining uncut)A small amount of wood on each side must remain uncut until the tree falls — the hinge wood is not all the wood between notch and back cut; a small holding wood margin should remain at the outside edge of the back cut.ANSI Z133; TCIA
⛔ Never Saw Through the Hinge

Cutting through the hinge — "barber chairing" — occurs when you over-run the back cut into the hinge wood. Once the hinge is severed, you lose all directional control. The tree can fall in any direction, and the butt can kick violently. Stop your back cut before the hinge. Use wedges to control lean if needed, not saw depth.

📋 Field Note — Okanagan Context

On steep-slope removal in the Okanagan — which describes a large percentage of residential felling above Kelowna, West Kelowna, Summerland, or Penticton — the hinge is doing extra work because the tree's center of gravity is often uphill. An undersized hinge on a back-leaning tree on a 30% slope is a high-consequence failure point. When in doubt on back-leaning trees, use a bore cut (plunge cut) technique: cut the back strap last, maintain full control of the remaining wood, and use wedges or a pull line to ensure controlled fall direction.
