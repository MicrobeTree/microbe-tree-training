📋 Pre-Felling Assessment Sequence

Per ANSI Z133 Section 4, a job hazard assessment must be completed before work begins. The sequence below is the industry-standard systematic approach. Work through it in order — every time, on every tree.

  • 1. Establish exclusion zone perimeter. Minimum radius = 2× tree height. Keep all non-essential personnel and public outside the zone before any assessment begins. ANSI Z133 §4.1 — No person shall be within the potential drop zone unless performing work that cannot be done elsewhere.
  • 2. Walk the full perimeter of the tree. Assess the tree from all four sides before committing to a felling direction. Do not assess from one angle only. Root plate, crown mass distribution, lean, and defects are only fully visible from multiple viewpoints.
  • 3. Assess the tree: species, size, lean, crown load, defects. Use the TRAQ/ISA tree risk assessment framework — identify structural defects, cavities, included bark, hanging branches. Record: DBH, estimated height, primary lean direction and severity, any significant defects at base, trunk, or crown.
  • 4. Identify hazards in the work zone. Overhead utilities (call BC One Call — 1-800-474-6886), structures, fences, vehicles, underground utilities, uneven terrain, slippery surfaces. Overhead conductors: ANSI Z133 §5.9 — minimum 3m approach distance for unqualified workers. Contact utility owner if within approach distance.
  • 5. Evaluate wind conditions. Assess current wind speed and direction. ANSI Z133 limits tree felling in winds exceeding 30 km/h. Check forecast — wind can change felling dynamics significantly. Gusty or variable wind in the Okanagan (Okanagan Connector corridor) is particularly hazardous — directional control is compromised.
  • 6. Determine felling direction. Based on lean assessment, drop zone analysis, and hazard locations. Identify primary and secondary (backup) felling directions. The felling direction should align with natural lean where possible — fighting lean requires more skill, more time, and carries higher risk.
  • 7. Clear and communicate escape routes. Establish and physically clear two escape routes — typically at 45° angles to the rear of the felling direction. Brief all crew on escape route locations. ANSI Z133 §4.3 — escape routes must be planned and cleared before felling begins. Minimum route length: 3m from stump. No stumps, branches, or debris in escape route.
  • 8. Position ground crew and confirm communication. All non-cutting crew members positioned outside the drop zone. Establish a clear verbal and visual communication protocol — one person directs, everyone knows the signal to move. ANSI Z133 — only the feller and a designated lookout may be in the work area during cutting. Lookout must be positioned to see both the crown and the feller.
  • 9. Check PPE. Faller: chainsaw chaps/pants, helmet with face shield and hearing protection, cut-resistant gloves, chainsaw boots. All crew: hardhat, hi-vis, appropriate footwear. WorkSafeBC OHS Regulation Part 26.6 — specific PPE requirements for chainsaw operations in BC.
  • 10. Saw inspection. Chain tension, bar condition, chain sharpness, chain brake function, throttle trigger, all guards in place, fuel level. Test chain brake before each use. A chain brake that fails to engage during kickback is a life-safety failure.

Interactive: tap each item to mark it complete. Use this as a real pre-task reference on site.

📋 Field Note — Okanagan Context

In the Okanagan, two hazards that get underestimated: First, the terrain. Many Okanagan tree jobs are on steep slopes — benched lots, rock faces, ravine edges — where "clear ground to the rear" doesn't exist. Plan escape routes uphill along the slope, not straight back. Second, overhead utilities. Kelowna and West Kelowna have extremely dense overhead utility corridors through residential areas — in-town tree work almost always involves utility proximity. BC One Call before any felling job. Always.

📐 Lean Assessment

Determining lean is the first step in felling direction selection. Lean is the dominant physical force acting on the tree during felling — it must be either used (by felling with the lean) or overcome (by notch placement, wedging, or mechanical assistance). Never ignore lean.

Front Lean
✅ Lean into drop zone
Felling direction matches lean direction. Most efficient and safest configuration. Hinge wood can be sized normally.
Back Lean
⚠️ Lean away from drop zone
Requires wedging or mechanical assistance to overcome. Never underestimate back lean — trees that "look straight" have more lean than you think. Use a plumb bob.
Side Lean
⚠️ Lean perpendicular to drop zone
Most insidious lean type — the tree will not fall where the notch points. Requires offset notch or significant mechanical correction. Plan carefully.
Balanced / No Lean
Crown mass assessment critical
A plumb trunk doesn't mean balanced. Check crown — heavy branches on one side create dynamic lean during fall. The crown hits first.
How to Measure Lean
  • Plumb bob method: Hang a plumb bob or level from an extended arm beside the trunk at chest height. Look up the trunk — the plumb line reveals lean direction and approximate angle. Best for subtle leans that aren't obvious visually.
  • Sight line method: Stand back (at least tree-height distance), close one eye, and align a vertical reference (fence post, building corner) with the base of the tree. Note how the top deviates from the reference.
  • Two-direction cross-check: Always check lean from two perpendicular positions — a tree may look straight from one angle but have significant side lean.
  • Crown mass vs. trunk lean: These can conflict. A tree with a straight trunk but heavy crown load to one side will behave as if it leans in that crown direction. The dominant factor may be the crown, not the trunk.
⚠️ Back Lean — The Most Dangerous Scenario
  • A back-leaning tree requires the notch to be cut deeper and the hinge to be sized very precisely — thin hinge = no control; too thick = tree doesn't tip
  • Wedging is required: use plastic or aluminum felling wedges (never steel into a running chain). Drive wedges before the back-cut reaches the hinge.
  • Mechanical assistance (come-along, winch, skidder) is the safest approach for significant back lean — preferably in combination with a pulling line attached well up in the tree
  • ANSI Z133 §8.3: "When using mechanical devices for felling, the device shall be of adequate capacity for the load and the anchor shall be capable of sustaining the expected loads."

📋 Field Note — Okanagan Slopes

Felling on Okanagan hillside lots — which is half the work in West Kelowna and Penticton — means the drop zone calculation must account for roll distance, not just fall distance. A 20m ponderosa on a 30° slope can send rounds rolling 50–80m downhill. Identify lower slope hazards (other structures, roads, vehicles) before felling and extend or reposition the exclusion zone to account for roll. On steep ground, cross-slope felling (falling the tree perpendicular to the slope) is often safer than felling downhill, even if it means working against a slight side lean.

🏃 Escape Routes — ANSI Z133 Requirements

Escape routes are not optional and are not improvised. They are planned, physically cleared, and communicated to the entire crew before the saw starts. Per ANSI Z133 §4.3: "Prior to felling, the arborist shall plan and clear at least two escape routes."

RequirementSpecification
Number of routesMinimum TWO escape routes, positioned to cover different movement options
DirectionApproximately 45° to the REAR of the felling direction — away from the direction the tree is falling and to the side
LengthMinimum 3m (10 ft) from the base of the stump, cleared and passable
ConditionPhysically cleared — no branches, stumps, ground debris, or trip hazards. The faller must be able to move quickly without looking down.
CommunicationBrief all crew on escape route locations before cutting. Every crew member near the work area must know where to go.
Usage triggerMove when the tree begins to fall — not when you hear a crack, not when you see the tree moving, but as a deliberate practiced motion at the moment the back-cut releases the hinge
🚫 Never Do These
  • Do not watch the tree fall while standing at the stump — move first, watch from a safe distance
  • Do not plan an escape route that goes toward the felling direction
  • Do not leave ground debris and branches in the escape route (a common and preventable mistake)
  • Do not assume you can dodge a misdirected tree — human reaction time is too slow. Pre-planned movement is the only reliable defense.
  • Do not position the escape route behind the stump in line with the tree — butt slap can throw the stump rearward
⚠️ What "Butt Slap" Means

As a tree falls forward, the butt can kick rearward before lifting off the stump — or a tree that splits or "barber chairs" (a vertical split up the trunk) can throw material backward toward the faller at high speed. The escape route at 45° to the rear specifically keeps the faller out of both the forward drop zone AND the rearward butt/barber-chair zone. This geometry is non-negotiable.

📋 Field Note — Make It Habit

Experienced fallers don't think about escape routes in the moment — they've done the pre-task setup so many times that movement is automatic. The point of clearing and briefing routes in advance is to remove decision-making from the most dangerous moment of the job. When the hinge releases and the tree starts moving, your legs should already know where to go. That only happens if you built the habit in low-stress moments first.

⚠️ Hazard Tree Indicators — Pre-Felling Checklist

Before felling any tree, systematically check for these hazard indicators. Each one changes the risk level and may require a modified approach, additional equipment, or reevaluation of felling direction.

  • Widow-makers / hung-up deadwood in crown. Look up — check for dead branches, hung-up wood from previous failures, or loose bark sections that will fall during cutting. ISA BMP: Any significant deadwood in the crown of a tree being felled should be removed by aerial means before the felling cut, or the work zone extended accordingly.
  • Root plate compromise. Check the base — heaved soil on one side, exposed roots, soil cracks radiating from the base, or leaning with root plate uplift visible. A compromised root plate means the tree may not fall where the notch points. Mechanical assistance is often required.
  • Cavities, decayed wood, or hollow sections in the trunk. A decayed hinge may not hold — the tree can go unpredictably, barber-chair, or fail before the back-cut is complete. Use a mallet to probe — a hollow sound indicates internal decay. Resistance drilling confirms. For severely decayed trees, consider sectional removal from the top.
  • Included bark at major unions. Included bark significantly reduces the structural strength of the union. A tree with included bark at the main fork may split unpredictably during felling rather than hinge normally.
  • Spring poles and tension wood. Leaning trees and trees grown under the suppression of a fallen neighbour can have severe internal tension in the wood. The saw can be pinched instantly and without warning. Boring cuts, wedging, and extreme care with cut sequencing are required on tension-loaded stems. One of the most common causes of saw pinch and operator injury.
  • Co-dominant stems / multiple leaders. Multi-stemmed trees may respond to one stem's fall by having the remaining stem move in an unexpected direction. Treat each stem as an independent felling event. Never stand in the fork of a co-dominant tree or within the swing arc of a remaining stem when felling one of a multi-stemmed tree.
  • Frozen or iced wood. Okanagan winters mean frozen wood in January-March — frozen wood is brittle and can split unpredictably rather than hinge cleanly. Hinge thickness should be increased in frozen conditions. TCIA Cold Weather Felling: frozen wood reduces hinge reliability. Use thicker-than-normal hinge, lower notch angle, and wedging assistance.
  • Adjacent trees, brush, or limbs that will catch the falling tree. A "hung tree" — one that lodges in an adjacent tree mid-fall — is one of the most dangerous situations in tree work. Never leave a hung tree unattended. ANSI Z133 §8.6: "A hung tree shall not be abandoned. The situation shall be corrected before work continues." Do not cut a hung tree's trunk from below.
