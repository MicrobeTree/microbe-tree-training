Bronze Birch Borer Agrilus anxius Gory
The wood-boring beetle that kills birch from the top down
🐛 Thursday — Pests & Diseases

The Bronze Birch Borer (BBB) is the most destructive insect pest of birch (Betula spp.) in western Canada. A native flatheaded borer in family Buprestidae, it is an opportunistic pathogen — healthy, well-irrigated birch in suitable sites resist attack; stressed trees succumb rapidly. Understanding its biology and diagnostic signs is essential for every arborist working in Okanagan urban and park settings, where paper and white birch are common ornamentals.

Question 1 of 5
What are the characteristic adult exit holes left by Bronze Birch Borer in the bark of an infested birch?
A
Round holes, 3–4 mm diameter, scattered randomly in the bark
B
D-shaped (flattened on one side) holes, 3–4 mm, in mid-crown to upper bole bark
C
Oval pitch tubes, 8–12 mm, with amber resin exudate
D
Irregular 1–2 cm ragged holes clustered at branch crotches
✔ Correct — B: D-shaped exit holes

Buprestid beetles (family Buprestidae) are called flatheaded borers because their larvae are dorso-ventrally flattened. They bore galleries through the phloem and outer xylem, and when adults emerge, they create the diagnostic D-shaped hole (flat on top, rounded below) that reflects the borer's body cross-section. This is the key field diagnostic for all Agrilus species, including Emerald Ash Borer.

Round holes indicate roundheaded borers (Cerambycidae) or Woodpeckers excavating for larvae. Pitch tubes indicate bark beetles (Scolytinae). Ragged holes suggest woodpecker feeding damage. The D-shape is definitive for buprestids.

Question 2 of 5
Crown dieback from Bronze Birch Borer infestation in white/paper birch typically progresses in which pattern?
A
From the base of the crown upward, with lower branch death first
B
Randomly distributed throughout the crown, with no predictable pattern
C
From the top of the crown downward, with upper branches dying first
D
Along one side of the crown (directional dieback toward sun exposure)
✔ Correct — C: Top-down dieback

BBB larvae feed in the phloem and cambium layer, girdling the vascular tissue. Female adults preferentially oviposit on sun-exposed upper stem and scaffold branch tissue where bark temperatures are highest — the beetle is thermophilic and attracted to warm, stressed bark. As larval galleries girdle progressively larger stem sections, water and nutrient transport fails in the upper crown first.

This top-down dieback pattern is called "flagging" in early stages (individual branches in the upper crown die while still retaining dried leaves) and progresses downward over 2–4 years until the entire tree is dead. By the time lower crown symptoms appear, infestation is typically severe. ISA exam note: this pattern also helps distinguish BBB from Verticillium wilt (which can cause sudden wilt of entire side branches at any crown position).

Question 3 of 5
Which birch species or cultural condition makes birch most susceptible to Bronze Birch Borer attack?
A
River Birch (Betula nigra) planted in full shade with consistent moisture
B
White/Paper Birch (Betula papyrifera) planted in hot, exposed urban sites with drought stress
C
European White Birch (Betula pendula) in its native Alpine range
D
Yellow Birch (Betula alleghaniensis) in cool, moist riparian settings
✔ Correct — B: Stressed paper birch in hot urban sites

Betula papyrifera (Paper/Canoe Birch) is the most susceptible species and is also the most commonly planted ornamental birch in Okanagan cities — a poor horticultural match for the hot, dry Interior climate. Paper birch is a cool-climate riparian species adapted to moist soils and cool summers; it is fundamentally out of its range in West Kelowna sun-baked boulevards.

Stressed trees produce volatiles (ethanol, terpenes) that BBB adults detect and use as host-selection cues. Drought stress is the primary predisposing factor. Betula nigra (River Birch) is notably more heat and BBB tolerant and is now recommended over paper birch for Interior BC plantings. Betula pendula (European White Birch) is intermediate in susceptibility. Yellow Birch in native moist sites is rarely attacked.

Question 4 of 5
In BC's Thompson-Okanagan region, when do Bronze Birch Borer adults typically emerge, mate, and oviposit?
A
March–April, concurrent with early spring leaf flush
B
Late May through early July, with peak activity in June
C
August–September during late summer heat
D
October–November just before dormancy
✔ Correct — B: Late May through early July

Adult BBB emerge when cumulative growing degree days (GDD, base 10°C) reach approximately 200–400. In the Okanagan, this corresponds to late May through late June in most years. Adults are active during warm, sunny days; they feed on birch leaves briefly before mating and ovipositing under bark crevices and lenticels.

This phenology window is critical for insecticide timing: preventive bark applications of emamectin benzoate or imidacloprid trunk injection must be applied before egg hatch and larval entry into phloem (June treatment window in Okanagan). Once larvae are feeding beneath bark, topical treatments are ineffective. The larvae overwinter as mature larvae and pupate in early spring. Life cycle is typically one year in warm Interior climates (vs. two years in cooler northern BC).

Question 5 of 5
An arborist is asked to manage a lightly infested white birch (10–15% crown dieback, D-shaped exit holes confirmed in upper bole). The tree is otherwise structurally sound, well-positioned, and the client wants to save it. What is the most appropriate integrated management approach?
A
Remove the tree immediately to prevent spread to adjacent birch
B
Inject imidacloprid into the soil now; repeat annually indefinitely
C
Improve tree vigour with deep-watering and mulching; apply targeted trunk injection (emamectin benzoate or imidacloprid) before next adult emergence window; remove dead wood; monitor annually
D
Spray the entire crown with pyrethroid insecticide to kill larvae feeding inside the bark
✔ Correct — C: Integrated management with vigour improvement

At 10–15% crown dieback, a vigorous management response can save the tree. The ISA and TCIA both recommend an integrated approach: (1) Address the predisposing stress first — deep root watering (slow drip, 3x/week in summer), apply 75–100 mm of organic mulch over the drip zone; (2) Remove dead and declining wood (reduces breeding habitat); (3) Apply a prophylactic trunk injection of emamectin benzoate (TREE-äge) or soil-applied imidacloprid timed 4–6 weeks before adult emergence; (4) Monitor annually for new exit holes.

Why the other options fail: Immediate removal is premature at this dieback level. Annual imidacloprid alone without addressing the root cause (drought stress) is not a long-term solution and raises pollinator concerns (linden is a bee forage tree). Pyrethroid sprays do not penetrate bark and have no efficacy against larvae feeding in the phloem. Trees with >50% crown dieback or extensive structural girdling in the lower bole are generally not viable candidates for treatment and should be removed.
