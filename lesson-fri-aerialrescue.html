Aerial Rescue Procedures
ANSI Z133 §9 protocols for incapacitated climber response
🚨 Friday — Worksite Techniques

⚠ When Aerial Rescue Protocol Activates

An aerial rescue is required any time a climber becomes incapacitated while suspended in a tree and cannot self-rescue. Incapacitation triggers include: loss of consciousness (cardiac event, heat stroke, anaphylaxis, bee sting reaction), physical injury (chainsaw cut, falling wood strike, broken limb failure), or suspension trauma (also called harness hang syndrome) — a life-threatening condition where a conscious but immobile climber sustains circulatory compromise from prolonged static hanging.

Every crew must have a written aerial rescue plan before any climbing operation begins — required by ANSI Z133-2017 §9.1 and reinforced by WorkSafeBC OHS Regulation Part 26. The plan must identify: the rescue type to be used, who is the designated rescuer, where rescue equipment is staged, and the EMS contact protocol.

⚡ Suspension Trauma — Time-Critical

A conscious climber hanging motionless in a harness can develop orthostatic hypotension and venous pooling within 5–30 minutes, leading to unconsciousness and cardiac arrest. If a climber reports leg numbness, weakness, or nausea while suspended — treat as an emergency. Get them moving or get them down immediately. Never have them hang motionless "to rest."

🦴 Rescue Types — Decision Framework

Type 1 — Self-Rescue
Climber descends under own power

Applicable when the climber is conscious, mobile, and capable. Climber activates their personal descent device (hitch cord lowering, SRS descent, or descender). Ground crew clears the drop zone and communicates continuously. Fastest option — always preferred if physically possible.

Type 2 — Assisted Lowering (Tender-Assisted)
Second climber or tender lowers the incapacitated climber

A second trained climber ascends or a ground tender uses a pre-rigged lowering line to bring the climber to the ground. Requires a pre-installed rescue line at the climber's working position or access to an adjacent line. The most common planned rescue method in tree work. Requires the ground crew to be trained in operating lowering devices under load.

Type 3 — Emergency Services Rescue
Fire/rescue services required

When no ground-based rescue option is available in an acceptable time frame, or the tree structure prevents a second climber from reaching the victim. Call 911 immediately. Ground crew maintains visual contact with the climber, reports condition to dispatch, and clears the work zone. Do NOT attempt a rescue beyond your training and equipment.

📋 Decision Rule

Time to rescue is the critical variable. Suspension trauma can become fatal in under 30 minutes. If Type 1 or Type 2 cannot be initiated within 10 minutes by a trained crew member, call 911 immediately — do not delay EMS while attempting a complex ground rescue beyond your team's competency.

🛠 Pre-Climb Rescue Setup (ANSI Z133 §9.3)

Rescue equipment must be staged and accessible before climbing begins — not retrieved from a locked truck box after an incident. Per ANSI Z133, the following must be established at every aerial operation:

Equipment Check
Rescue Line Ready

A second rope (rescue line) of adequate length is pre-installed in the tree or immediately available. For DdRT systems, the working line can double as rescue line if second anchor point is set.

Equipment Check
Lowering Device Staged

A friction device (Portawrap, GRCS, Figure-8, or rescue Munter) capable of handling the climber's weight is at the base of the tree, ready to use, not packed away.

Communication
Signals Established

Verbal, whistle, or radio signals agreed upon pre-climb. Ground crew knows the distress signal vs. normal work signals. Written in the rescue plan.

Personnel
Rescuer Designated

A trained crew member is identified as primary rescuer before climbing begins. They do not simultaneously operate the chipper or other isolating equipment during the climb.

EMS
Address & Access Confirmed

Job site address, cross street, and access route confirmed and written down. Who calls 911 is pre-assigned. EMS access to the site pre-checked (locked gates, narrow laneways).

PPE
First Aid Kit Staged

Comprehensive first aid kit including trauma dressings, tourniquet, and epinephrine autoinjector (if carried). First aid trained crew member identified.

📋 Rescue Protocol — Step by Step

When a climber is observed to be incapacitated or unresponsive:

1
Call out & assess

Call the climber by name loudly. Establish if conscious and responsive. If no response, initiate rescue immediately — do not delay to assess injuries from the ground.

2
Assign roles

One crew member is the rescuer and focuses entirely on the rescue. A second crew member calls 911 and stays on with dispatch. Everyone else clears the drop zone and maintains crowd control.

3
Establish control of victim's line

Locate the climber's rope. Install the lowering device (friction hitch or mechanical device) on the rope. If the victim is on a life-safety device (SRS), the rescuer must understand how to actuate descent — this is system-specific and must be trained in advance.

4
Lower to ground under control

Lower the victim smoothly at a controlled rate. Communicate with EMS on location and ETD. Ground crew supports the victim during the final 2 m of descent — do not let an unconscious climber impact the ground.

5
Position victim & begin first aid

For unconscious climber: check airway, breathing, circulation. Do not remove the harness if you suspect spinal injury from a fall. If victim is conscious after suspension trauma: keep them moving (walking if possible), never lay them flat immediately — gradual position change prevents re-perfusion cardiac event.

6
Transfer to EMS & document

Brief EMS on mechanism of incident, time suspended, interventions given. Secure the site. Preserve equipment for investigation. Complete WorkSafeBC incident report within 24 hours for any injury requiring medical attention. Notify your employer immediately per WCB OHS Regulation.

📡 Communication Signals

Establish pre-agreed signals before each climb. Standard ANSI Z133 / TCIA-recommended rope signal system (when verbal communication is obstructed by equipment noise):

1 tug

Stop / Hold — do not move the rope

2 tugs

Give slack / lower slowly

3 tugs

Take up slack / lift

Continuous tugging

Emergency / distress — initiate rescue

Rope signals only work on taut lines. On slack-rope systems (SRS), radio communication or a spotter with visual line-of-sight is required. Confirm your system before climbing.

📝 Post-Incident Requirements

RequirementDetail
WorkSafeBC ReportingSerious injury: notify WorkSafeBC immediately (1-888-621-7233); written report within 24 hours. Minor injury: record in first aid book.
Equipment InspectionAll life-safety equipment involved in the rescue (ropes, harnesses, carabiners) must be removed from service and inspected or retired before any return to work.
Incident InvestigationEmployer must investigate root cause. Crew debrief within 24 hours while events are fresh. Identify corrective actions.
Critical Incident SupportAerial rescue and trauma response cause psychological stress. Arrange CISM (Critical Incident Stress Management) debrief for crew within 24–72 hours.
Rescue Plan ReviewUpdate the aerial rescue plan based on lessons learned before returning to work.
📋 Field Note — Okanagan

In the Okanagan, EMS response times to urban Kelowna are typically 8–12 minutes. Rural West Kelowna or hillside sites can be 15–20+ minutes. This means your crew must be capable of executing a Type 2 rescue independently — you cannot rely on fire rescue arriving in time if the climber is in suspension trauma. At Microbe Tree, every crew member with climbing duties must complete the ISA Tree Worker Aerial Rescue practical training (ANSI Z133 §9 hands-on component) before their first solo aerial operation. Staged rescue drills minimum once per season keep skills sharp.
