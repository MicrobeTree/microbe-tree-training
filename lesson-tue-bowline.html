冒聼陋垄 Tying the Bowline
Bowline tying sequence 芒聙聰 four steps to a fixed loop. Always dress and set before loading.
  • Make the loop (the "rabbit hole"). Create a small overhand loop in the standing part of the rope 芒聙聰 cross right over left if looking from the end. The crossing point should be about 30cm from where you want your final fixed loop to sit.
  • Bring the working end up through the loop. The working end is the "rabbit." Come up through the hole from below 芒聙聰 this is the only direction that produces a bowline.
  • Pass the working end around the standing part. The rabbit runs around the "tree" (the standing part). Keep the working end on the correct side 芒聙聰 this is where most tying errors occur. The end should pass behind the standing part and come back toward the loop.
  • Feed the working end back down through the loop and dress. The rabbit goes back down the hole. Pull all four strands in opposite pairs to dress the knot 芒聙聰 this is critical. An undressed bowline is dramatically weaker and can invert.
  • Set with a backup. Per ANSI Z133, all knots in climbing systems must be secured. Add an overhand or half-hitch backup with the tail 芒聙聰 minimum 15cm tail length after the backup knot.

芒職聽茂赂聫 Load Direction Matters

The bowline is designed to be loaded along the standing part. Side-loading the loop (loading perpendicular to the standing part) significantly degrades the knot and can cause it to collapse. When rigging, ensure the load is always pulling along the standing part axis.

芒聹聟 When to Use the Bowline

The bowline is a foundational arborist knot valued for its predictability, ease of release after loading, and wide application range. Proper use means matching the bowline to tasks where its properties align with the load requirements.

芒聹聟 Appropriate Uses
  • Throwline retrieval loops 芒聙聰 bowline at the end of the throwline for weight attachment
  • Temporary anchor points 芒聙聰 securing a rope to a fixed anchor (rock, trunk, bollard) where a fixed loop is needed
  • Securing loads on the truck 芒聙聰 when a fixed non-slip loop on a rope is needed for lashing
  • Attaching ropes to rigging hardware 芒聙聰 when a carabiner or shackle is not available and a fixed loop connection is needed
  • Rescue lowering systems 芒聙聰 where a fixed loop seat or attachment point is constructed from rope
  • General utility 芒聙聰 anywhere a reliable, non-slip, easily released fixed loop is needed and no dynamic or cyclic load is expected
冒聼聯聥 Field Note 芒聙聰 Okanagan Context

In Okanagan tree work, the bowline shows up most often in two places: on throwlines (looped around the weight) and as a quick temporary anchor when you're doing a ground-supported rigging setup and need to secure a redirect. It's the first knot a new crew member should be able to tie with eyes closed, in gloves, one-handed 芒聙聰 because those are the conditions you'll be in when you need it. 冒聼職芦 When NOT to Use the Bowline
冒聼職芦 Do Not Use the Bowline For:
  • Primary climbing system connection (harness tie-in) 芒聙聰 The bowline is not a rated, certified climbing system connection method. Use a figure-8 follow-through or a rated connector per ANSI Z133. The bowline lacks the positive locking of a threaded sleeve carabiner or sewn connection.
  • Any application subject to cyclic or oscillating loads 芒聙聰 The bowline can progressively loosen and invert under repeated load/unload cycles. A backed bowline under steady load is acceptable; a bowline on a rope that is repeatedly jerked or bounced can work loose over time.
  • As a friction hitch 芒聙聰 The bowline does not grip the standing line and will not function as a friction hitch for positioning or descent. Use a Prusik, VT hitch, or Blake's hitch for those applications.
  • In shock-load scenarios without a backup 芒聙聰 An unbacked bowline under sudden shock load can fail or invert. Always back it.
  • When an adjustable loop is needed 芒聙聰 The bowline loop is fixed. Use a truckers hitch or clove hitch where adjustability is required.

Per ANSI Z133 Section 5.3: All knots used in climbing systems must be the correct type for the application and must be secured (backed). The standard does not prohibit specific knots by name but requires that the knot be appropriate for the intended load and application 芒聙聰 meaning it is the arborist's responsibility to understand knot limitations and match them to the task.

冒聼聯聥 Field Note 芒聙聰 The Honest Truth About the Bowline

The bowline's reputation is bigger than its rating. It's one of the most recognized knots in the world 芒聙聰 which means it gets used in situations it shouldn't be. In arboriculture, the figure-8 follow-through is the go-to for life-safety end-of-line connections because it's harder to tie wrong and easier to visually inspect. The bowline is excellent for what it's excellent for. Know the difference before you're 25 metres up.

冒聼聰聮 Securing the Bowline 芒聙聰 ANSI Z133 Compliance

ANSI Z133 (Safe Work Practices for Arborists) requires that all knots used in climbing systems be secured. For the bowline, the standard backup is a half-hitch or overhand knot tied with the working tail around the loop leg (the leg of the loop parallel to the standing part).

The half-hitch backup is tied with the working tail around the loop leg of the bowline. Tail must be at least 15cm after the backup.
  • Tie and dress the bowline. Fully dress the knot 芒聙聰 pull all four strands to seat it properly before adding the backup.
  • Identify the loop leg parallel to the standing part. This is the leg the backup goes around 芒聙聰 not the working tail itself, and not the standing part.
  • Tie a half-hitch or overhand with the working tail around the parallel loop leg. The tail wraps around and through the resulting loop.
  • Snug the backup tight against the bowline body. No gap between the backup and the main knot 芒聙聰 this is what prevents inversion.
  • Check tail length 芒聙聰 minimum 15cm. If the tail is shorter, the knot is undersized for the application or was tied with insufficient rope end.

  • Backup is tied and snugged against the knot body
  • Working tail is minimum 15cm past the backup

  • Load direction is along the standing part axis
